Moral skepticism (or moral scepticism) is a [class](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Class_(philosophy) "Class (philosophy)") of [metaethical](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Meta-ethics "Meta-ethics") theories in which all members entail that no one has any moral knowledge. Many moral skeptics also make the stronger, [modal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Modal_logic "Modal logic") claim that [moral knowledge is impossible](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moral_nihilism "Moral nihilism"). Moral skepticism is particularly against [moral realism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moral_realism "Moral realism") which holds the view that there are knowable and objective moral truths.[_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_]

Some proponents of moral skepticism include [Pyrrho](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pyrrho "Pyrrho"), [Aenesidemus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aenesidemus "Aenesidemus"), [Sextus Empiricus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sextus_Empiricus "Sextus Empiricus"), [David Hume](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/David_Hume "David Hume"), [Max Stirner](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Max_Stirner "Max Stirner"), [Friedrich Nietzsche](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Friedrich_Nietzsche "Friedrich Nietzsche"), and [J.L. Mackie](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/J._L._Mackie "J. L. Mackie").[_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_]
